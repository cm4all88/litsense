THE PROBLEM WE COULDN'T IGNORE

Most book recommendation tools are built for volume. Rate enough books and a machine will guess what you might tolerate next. Browse long enough and you'll find something to buy.

That's not a reading life. That's a queue.

The best book recommendation you ever got probably came from someone who knew you — who remembered that you cried at the ending of a novel three years ago, who understood that you needed something slow and anchoring this particular winter, who didn't just ask what you liked but why.

LitSense is an attempt to build that.

Every reader carries a private map of what books have meant to them. We built a companion that actually reads it.

HOW WE THINK ABOUT RECOMMENDATIONS

A book isn't just its genre. It's pace and texture and emotional temperature. It's what you need from a story right now, which is different from what you needed six months ago. It's the specific kind of melancholy you find comforting, the particular register of prose that makes you feel like you're being spoken to rather than performed at.

LitSense tracks all of it — not as data points, but as a portrait. Your shelves, your moods, your reading history, your idle questions to Sage at eleven o'clock on a Tuesday. Everything feeds a picture of you as a reader that gets sharper over time.

The result is recommendations that feel less like suggestions and more like recognition.

WHAT WE BELIEVE

TASTE OVER TRAFFIC

We don't surface books because they're popular. We surface them because they're right for you, right now.

DEPTH OVER VOLUME

One book that genuinely changes you is worth a hundred you finish and forget. We optimize for that one.

COMPANION, NOT ENGINE

Sage isn't a search bar. She's a reader who has thought carefully about what might matter to you.

YOUR READING IS YOURS

We don't sell your reading life to advertisers. Your shelf is private. Your preferences power your experience, not someone else's.

SAGE

Every reader deserves a companion who pays attention. Sage is ours.

She remembers what you've loved and what left you cold. She notices when your mood shifts. She asks better questions than "what genre do you like?" — because that question has never captured anything true about the way people actually read.

You can tell Sage you want something that feels like autumn in a city you've never been to, or that you need a novel that trusts you to keep up, or that you just finished something devastating and need to breathe for a while. She understands. She'll find it.
